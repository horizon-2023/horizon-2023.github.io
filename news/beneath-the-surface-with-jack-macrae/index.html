beneath the surface with jack macrae

Originally, I started with collages, but unfortunately had to progress from there due to weather screwing with the paper. Now I mainly paint. It’s very formalist and paired back. Your style is extremely unique. I haven‚Äôt seen anything that looks like your work, which is nice ‚Äî it‚Äôs very Jack MacRae. How did you land on your style? And why the collages? Why these bold colors?

Jack MacRae: So, there’s probably a few different things that led to it. It’s a well thought out process but one day it just clicked. I’d worked for a lot of different architects who only believed in hand drawing, hand building, hand creating, because doing stuff on the computer, you get stuck in a version of Atari World ‚Äî it’s not real and you lose a sense of scale. So, that sort of knowledge transferred through to my practice, where I had to hand create things. In my final years of architecture, we studied modernism architecture, and formalism, and the syntax of architecture. One day I just created a Wollumbin A5 collage that was four or five colours. It was a very simple. I hand cut it, glued it together, and just went, ‚ÄúWow, this is super effective!‚Äù. I really liked it. I like to keep it as simple as possible and be able to recreate it over and over again, ensuring it‚Äôs all handmade and as special as possible. Everyone has a favorite landscape, and everyone has favorite colours, so everyone can respond to it.

Yeah, for sure. I’ve spoken to you about this in the past ‚Äî about how whenever you have a spare minute, all you think about doing, and all you want to do, is just come up to this room and paint [we are sat in Jack‚Äôs upstairs studio]. Why do you feel the need to block yourself out from the outside world?

I think it’s just a case of trialing different things. I mean, at the end of the day, I might not be in the most creative job, but it might just be me wanting to create and try new things, new color palettes, different landscapes, test out new methods of painting and see what happens. And just being able to immerse myself in this space and sit there with this huge canvas in front of me ‚Äî and go face to face with it ‚Äî is something that I feel the need to do.

I’m definitely open to evolving my art because at the end of the day, everything should evolve ‚Äî change is always good. I definitely go through stages of trial and error. There are some things that might not work, but it‚Äôs just a case of sitting down and just trialing new things that I haven’t done before. It might not be techniques, but it could be the things that I want to paint ‚Äî like doing a striped painting as opposed to a collage.

Do you see imitation as a form flattery or would it bother you if you started seeing your sort of stuff elsewhere?

I’m lucky that I haven’t seen much imitation at this stage, so I’m very happy with that. But I would also be highly flattered unless, again, someone was profiting and doing way better than I was [laughs]. But, at the end of the day, it could be two train of thoughts that have come from two different places to end up with the very similar results. So, you can’t get too annoyed. It’s a tough one.

Are you trying to tell the audience anything through your work? Is there a message that’s flowing through all your pieces? Or is it as simple as: ‚ÄòI reckon these landscapes are rad and I love the colors?‚Äô.

I think it’s as simple as that. I don’t really have a message for people. Generally, my pieces are quite happy. There‚Äôs not much doom and gloom and hopefully what I paint can bring happiness and joy to others.

I love that. Your artworks are you in canvas form.

Yeah, well hopefully. I guess it’s painting that makes me happy or doing the art that makes me happy. So, hopefully that comes through.

Let‚Äôs chat about your full-time job. You work as a draftsperson ‚Äî was that always something you wanted to do?

I was very fortunate as I was always able to say, ‚ÄúI think I want to do architecture.‚Äù I’ve always been in love with this Geoffrey Pie house in Peregian ‚Äî my cousin‚Äôs one. And there’s a few other Geoffrey Pie houses in Peregian, which are just like, phenomenal! They don’t age, or, if they do, they age with character. They’re extremely vernacular, well-built houses. At 15-years old I thought, ‚ÄúI want to create houses like that‚Äù. And then all of a sudden, I ended up going down this really long path of architecture. I absolutely love it. I think architecture paves the way for the rest of the world.

Your architecture practice seems so methodical ‚Äî everything seems so straight and thought-out. Yet your creative practice seems like the polar opposite ‚Äî It’s so malleable, it’s free flowing, it seems like the total opposite. Is your artwork the balancing point in your life?

They’re both pretty much full-time jobs. One of them is paid quite consistently and the other one ebbs and flows. And if you can get both working at the exact same time, it’s fantastic.

Yeah, absolutely. If I can have somewhere to go and explore, be outdoors, go to the beach, that drives me to work creatively. Living at Fingal, I would go and watch the sunset at Chinderah, over the Tweed River, and just watch Mount Wollumbin most afternoons. And it’s such a similar feeling with Mount Coolum as well. For some weird reason, giant rocks with giant mountains just give me a sense of feeling like home, which is awesome.

Yeah, so bodysurfing is hopefully my next career path [laughs]. I’ve always bodysurfed as a kid. Dad would say, ‚ÄúGo out and go for a swim!‚Äù or we’d just throw ourselves into shore breaks on days that we couldn’t surf. And when it was too big, we’d just go out and swim.

Unreal.

When I moved to Fingal, there was this epic little wedge and my good friend Rory and I decided one year when we first lived there, ‚ÄúHey, let’s have a bodysurfing competition. 10 bucks entry, whoever wants to be involved, winner takes all.‚Äù Rory smashed it the first year and the second year. And then, in our third year, we had our Wollumbin Womp Comp. We’d been partying for two or three days beforehand because it was like a bit of an Easter joke. Anyway, this one year, we get a phone call and there’s people down there waiting to watch! It’s like seven in the morning, we‚Äôre all hungover, and we‚Äôre like, ‚ÄúWhat the hell is going on!?‚Äù. The surf was pumping ‚Äî like four foot out the back ‚Äî and the current world champion was down there! [laughs]

His name is Tom Marr. His whole family were there. They had set up marquees, and we were just like, ‚ÄúOh my God, we are so under prepared for this competition.‚Äù We were the one of the evolving bodysurfing competitions of the time.

Hahah, yeah. I‚Äôm going over for the bodysurfing world champs. I‚Äôve got a fair bit of training to do.

Ok, this is quickly become a Tracks feature. Let‚Äôs chat about some of the deeper stuff. When I look at you, you seem like this handsome, happy dude. Is there anything beneath the surface that you struggle with?

I‚Äôm seeing a common theme. Your work feels very structured. You have work goals. But your creative practice is free flowing. There are no goals.
